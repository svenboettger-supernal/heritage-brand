# Heritage brand prompt

Standalone brand brief for an AI tool building a Heritage-themed surface — the Heritage Strategies LLC firm site, an investor or client deck, a quarterly letter, a wealth-summary PDF, a private placement memorandum, a family-office report, a trust schedule, a portfolio statement, an internal advisor tool, or any artifact representing Heritage externally. Apply these values directly — no token system or design-system install required.

---

## Brand foundation — read this before touching a color or a font

Heritage Strategies LLC is a financial advisory firm at the family-office / private-bank tier. The brand is **not** about asset allocation or financial planning; it is about holding things in trust across generations the principal will not live to see. Every design decision answers to that promise.

- **Archetype:** Custodian / Trustee / Steward. Discreet, archival, long-tenure, generational. Never Disruptor, never Performer, never Lifestyle-Influencer, never Innovator.
- **Enemy:** the clinical retail-bank UI (Chase blue, Wells gold-on-stadium-photo, brokerage app charts on white), the "we believe in YOU" marketing voice, stock-photo wealth advisors in glass towers, animated counters that report AUM, "experience true wealth" headlines. Heritage positions against all of it. Restraint is the differentiator.
- **Posture:** archival, generational, discreet. Closer to an estate-planning attorney's letterhead, the inside of a probate file, the title page of a private placement memorandum, or the binding of a leather-bound minute book than to a financial-services website. Confidence reads as quietude; the brand is the **stewardship**, not the strategy.
- **The unifying brief:** be the most credible voice in private wealth by behaving like a trustee writing a quarterly letter, not a marketer pitching a product.

If a design decision makes the brand louder, faster, glossier, or more obviously "innovative," it is wrong — regardless of how well-executed it is in isolation. The audience for Heritage has seen every trendy brand cycle; they are allergic to all of them. Slow design ages well. Slow design is the brand.

---

## Output context — classify before you apply anything

Read the user's request and decide which output you are building:

- **Marketing surface** — public-facing pages for heritagestrategies.com; investor decks; one-page tear sheets; client-facing PDFs (quarterly letters, family-office briefings, wealth-strategy summaries, trust-formation memos). **Editorial mode applies in full.** Skip status colors and form/input chrome unless the page actually contains a form.
- **App or prototype (advisor / family-office tooling)** — the working software used by Heritage advisors, trustees, and family principals: asset registers, distribution schedules, beneficiary maps, document vaults, audit trails. **All sections apply, including status colors and tabular-figure rules.**
- **Internal document** — Notion-style notes, decision memos, ops docs, deal post-mortems, family meeting prep. Apply only typography, page surfaces, and surface neutrals. No buttons, no status badges, no charts unless the content needs them.
- **Document of record** — letterhead, formal letter to a principal, executed agreement cover. Apply only the mark, typography, page surfaces, and the bronze hairline. No buttons, no badges, no decorative motifs beyond the wax seal in its letterhead position.

If unsure, ask before applying status colors or form/input guidance. **Never invent UI controls — buttons, badges, alerts, status indicators, forms — just to apply the colors below.** Use only what the content actually requires.

## Aesthetic

Editorial, heraldic, archival — screens read like the cover of a private placement memorandum, the title page of a leather-bound minute book, the front of a quarterly letter from a trustee. Restraint as the organizing principle: one deep navy ground, one wax-seal identity accent used as a signature (not a UI color), one bronze hairline that frames the page, parchment reserved for document surfaces, a single luminous arc that signs the page like a watermark.

Generous negative space on the dark ground. Hierarchy comes from scale, spacing, and the bronze hairlines that crown and close the layout — not from heavy color contrast or busy chrome. Type does the heavy lifting; the wax-seal motif and the luminous arc are the only meaningful pieces of ornament, and even they appear once per viewport at most.

Asymmetric balance over centered stacks. The compositions a trustee would recognize as serious: a single coat-of-arms or seal at the upper-left, a measured rule beneath the title, the body of the letter beginning where it always begins. Nothing that signals "marketing." Nothing that signals "fintech." Nothing that signals "luxury" as an adjective.

The site of a firm at this tier should age slowly on purpose. Avoid every signal that will look dated in five years — gradients beyond the navy ground itself, glassmorphism, illustrated mascots, animated counters, marketing-pop motion, urgency banners, "Schedule a Discovery Call" pop-ups, chatbots, AUM tickers, sunset-on-mountain hero photography, handshakes-in-glass-towers stock.

## The mark

Heritage's identity mark is the **wax-seal HS monogram** paired with the **HERITAGE / STRATEGIES LLC** Trajan-style serif lockup, with a thin bronze hairline running between the two lines of the wordmark. Treat it as a family crest pressed into wax, not a tech logo.

### The seal

A soft circular wax disc, pressed (not flat-drawn). The fill is the **Wax Seal Oxblood** identity color. The "HS" lettering inside is set in a classical serif (Trajan or Tiempos Headline weight), in a deeper seal-shadow color, with the slight indentation of a true seal impression — not a vector outline. The edge of the seal is irregular, the way wax cools — never a perfect circle, never a beveled gradient ring, never a glossy 3D lens.

- **Never recolor the seal.** It is wax-oxblood on every brand surface. Do not flatten to one color, do not invert to white, do not apply gradients beyond the natural shading of wax. If a single-color treatment is required (a fax cover, a foil stamp on linen card), use a solid Wax Seal Oxblood silhouette, never a tinted variant.
- **Never set the seal inside a containing shape.** No box, no ring, no card behind it. The seal sits directly on the page.
- **Never animate the seal.** It does not spin, pulse, fade in, or "stamp down" on page load.

### The wordmark

Two lines: **HERITAGE** stacked above **STRATEGIES LLC**, set in the display serif at the same x-axis, with a **single thin bronze hairline** running horizontally between the two lines for the width of "HERITAGE." This horizontal rule is part of the wordmark — never omit it, never thicken it, never recolor it.

- The seal sits to the **left** of the wordmark on horizontal lockups, aligned with the bronze hairline.
- "HERITAGE" is the top line in display serif, all-caps, regular weight (not bold), default letter-spacing.
- "STRATEGIES LLC" is the bottom line in the same display serif, all-caps, regular weight, slightly tighter visual size to match the cap-height visually.
- The bronze hairline is **1 stroke wide at any reproduction size** — a true hairline. On screen at 16px wordmark height it renders as 1px; at 200px it renders as 1.5–2px maximum.

### Pairing rules

- **Two-color treatment, always.** Wax Seal Oxblood seal + Cream wordmark on dark grounds. Wax Seal Oxblood seal + Deep Navy wordmark on light grounds. Never paint the entire lockup oxblood. Never paint the entire lockup white. Never apply gradients to the lockup.
- **Minimum sizes.** Full lockup (seal + wordmark) ≥ 180px wide. Seal alone (favicon, social avatar, document footer) ≥ 32px square. Below that, use a hand-set "HS" monogram in the display serif — never a downscaled raster of the seal (the wax detail disappears and reads as a smudge).
- **Clear space.** Reserve the **height of the "H" in HERITAGE** around the entire lockup. Nothing crosses that boundary — not a luminous arc, not a hairline, not adjacent type, not a corner ornament.
- **Never alter.** The lockup is never stretched, rotated, recolored, outlined, embossed beyond the seal's natural impression, drop-shadowed, or set inside a containing shape (no "logo in a circle," no "logo in a square card," no "logo in a tab").

## Typography

Two families do all the work — **never more than two fonts in a single design**. Classical display serif for the wordmark, headings, pull quotes, and editorial body on letterhead. Clean modern sans for navigation, UI, dense data, and screen body. Add JetBrains Mono for account references, file numbers, and code.

| Role | Family | Weights | Used for |
|------|--------|---------|----------|
| Display serif | **Tiempos Headline** (Klim) / **Mercury Text Std** (Hoefler) — fallback: **Cormorant Garamond** (Google Fonts) | 400, 500, 500 italic | Wordmark, h1, h2, hero copy, pull quotes, letterhead salutation |
| Sans (UI + body) | **Söhne** (Klim) / **GT America Standard** (Grilli Type) — fallback: **Inter** (Google Fonts) | 400, 500, 600 | h3–h6, body, navigation, captions, tables, all dense data |
| Mono | JetBrains Mono | 400 | Account refs, trust numbers, file identifiers, code |

If brand-licensed Tiempos / Söhne are available, use them. If not, **Cormorant Garamond and Inter are mandatory free fallbacks** — they are not "good enough," they are part of the brand system. The two pairings produce visually adjacent results because both serifs share Garamond ancestry and both sans-serifs share humanist proportions. Alternatives (Times New Roman, Georgia, Arial) are document-of-record substitutes only — acceptable in a Word doc or an Outlook email, never on a web surface.

Load via Google Fonts or your framework's font system. Set `font-display: swap`. Never use raw `<link>` tags or `@import` in a component file when a framework font loader is available.

```css
/* Fallback stack — when Tiempos / Söhne licenses are unavailable */
@import url('https://fonts.googleapis.com/css2?family=Cormorant+Garamond:ital,wght@0,400;0,500;0,600;1,400;1,500&family=Inter:wght@300;400;500;600;700&family=JetBrains+Mono:wght@400&display=swap');

:root {
  --font-display: 'Tiempos Headline', 'Mercury Text Std', 'Cormorant Garamond', Georgia, serif;
  --font-sans:    'Söhne', 'GT America', 'Inter', system-ui, -apple-system, sans-serif;
  --font-mono:    'JetBrains Mono', 'SF Mono', ui-monospace, monospace;
}
```

### Heading + Subheading pattern (signature device)

Heritage's signature type lockup is a **serif HEADING in cream or wax oxblood** stacked directly above a **sans SUBHEADING in bronze small-caps**, separated by a 1px bronze hairline running the width of the subheading. Reproduce it exactly where a section opens — a card, a panel, a deck slide, a PDF section break.

```html
<header class="section-title">
  <p class="section-title__eyebrow">CHAPTER · 02</p>
  <div class="section-title__rule"></div>
  <h2 class="section-title__heading">Stewardship across generations</h2>
  <p class="section-title__subheading">The trustee's quarterly letter, rendered for the page</p>
</header>
```

```css
.section-title__eyebrow {
  font-family: var(--font-display);
  font-size: 12px;
  font-weight: 500;
  letter-spacing: 0.18em;
  text-transform: uppercase;
  color: var(--brand-bronze);
  margin: 0 0 12px 0;
}
.section-title__rule {
  width: 64px;
  height: 1px;
  background: var(--brand-bronze);
  margin-bottom: 28px;
}
.section-title__heading {
  font-family: var(--font-display);
  font-size: 48px;
  font-weight: 400;
  line-height: 1.1;
  letter-spacing: -0.005em;
  color: var(--text-strong);
  margin: 0 0 16px 0;
}
.section-title__subheading {
  font-family: var(--font-sans);
  font-size: 20px;
  font-weight: 500;
```

### Letter-spacing

- Display serif headings: tighten slightly at large sizes (`-0.005em` at 36px+, `-0.01em` at 64px+). Never tighten body serif.
- Bronze small-caps eyebrows: `letter-spacing: 0.18em`.
- Body and UI: default tracking.
- Mono (account references, trust numbers): default tracking, uppercase.

### Numerals — non-negotiable in private wealth

Heritage's product is full of money. Every numeric value in a table, schedule, chart, distribution box, portfolio statement, or PDF must use **tabular figures**:

```css
font-variant-numeric: tabular-nums;
font-feature-settings: "tnum" 1;
```

Apply on `table`, `.sheet`, `.statement`, `.schedule`, `.amount`, `pre`, `code`, and any data-bearing component. Prose numbers (e.g. "across four generations") use the default proportional figures and are fine.

For editorial body in the display serif (quarterly letters, family-office prose), prefer **old-style figures** (`onum`) — they sit naturally in a serif paragraph and signal the editorial register:

```css
font-feature-settings: "onum" 1;
```

Switch to lining figures only when numbers appear in tabular contexts within the same document.

### Type rules

- **Maintain a clean and professional hierarchy in text sizes.** Three sizes per page maximum for prose; one display, one body, one caption.
- **Avoid using more than two fonts per design.** Display serif + sans. That is the entire system. JetBrains Mono is allowed for account references, code, and file numbers — it does not count as a third font for the rule above, but use it only where it earns its place.
- **Keep text contrast high.** On the deep navy ground, body text is `#d4d8df` Stone Mist (not white-at-low-opacity). On parchment, body text is `#0c1a2b` Deep Midnight.

### Strict role partitioning — read this twice

Heritage has seven swatches. They are strictly partitioned. **The system breaks the moment any color crosses its lane.**

1. **Deep Midnight Navy (`#0e1d33`)** — the page. Everything sits on it. Never used as a button fill, never used as a text color (we use the foreground stops below for that).
2. **Wax Seal Oxblood (`#9a4b3a`)** — **identity, not interaction.** Use for the wax seal, the active-nav-item underline, a single section-opening pip on a quarterly letter, foil-stamp accents on print. **Never paint a button oxblood.** Never paint a chart series oxblood. Oxblood is the family crest — it dilutes the moment it becomes a generic UI color.
3. **Bronze Hairline (`#a87f4b`)** — **structural rule color.** The hairline between HERITAGE and STRATEGIES LLC, the rule beneath the nav, the eyebrow color, the underline beneath a section title. **Never a fill color.** Never a button background. Never a chart series. Bronze is metallic ink, used in thin lines only.
4. **Parchment Cream (`#f4ede0`)** — **document interiors only.** Letterhead, PDF body pages, print pages. Never used as a marketing-page ground — that role belongs to Deep Midnight Navy.
5. **Stone Mist (`#d4d8df`)** — body text on dark. Quiet dividers. The wordmark on dark. Not an action color, not a fill.
6. **Quiet Slate (`#091525`)** — cards and panels on the navy ground. The convention is **cards descend toward black, not ascend toward white** — like ink absorbing into paper.
7. **Steel Cobalt (`#3d5d8a`)** — **state only.** Focus rings, active tabs, "currently selected" indicators, info status. The moment cobalt appears as a decorative element, its meaning as a state indicator is broken everywhere else.

### Page surfaces

The default Heritage surface is **dark**. The brand was designed for the deep navy ground from the wax-seal Zoom card forward — light surfaces are the exception, used for documents that will be printed (letterhead, quarterly letters, PPMs) or for legibility-critical pages (long-form regulatory disclosures, dense schedules).

| Role | Value | Where |
|------|-------|-------|
| Editorial dark (default page background) | `hsl(216 57% 13%)` — `#0e1d33` | Default for every Heritage marketing surface, deck, and screen-bound PDF |
| Card / panel on dark | `hsl(216 60% 9%)` — `#091525` | Cards and panels sit one step deeper than the page, not lighter |
| Card hover / elevated panel on dark | `hsl(216 55% 11%)` | A barely-perceptible lift |
| Editorial light (document interior, letterhead, print) | `hsl(40 50% 92%)` — `#f4ede0` | Warm parchment, never pure white. Pure white is for the inside of the lockup containment box only |
| Card on light | `hsl(0 0% 100%)` | Pure white sits on the parchment with a quiet hue gap |
| Sidebar / muted ground (light mode, app) | `hsl(40 38% 88%)` | App sidebar, secondary surfaces on parchment |

The page is deep navy; cards on dark go **darker, not lighter**. This is the opposite of most SaaS conventions and is core to the Heritage feel — the layout reads like ink-on-paper or oil-on-canvas, not pixels-on-screen.

### Status colors (app / prototype mode only)

**Skip this section entirely if you are building a marketing surface, deck, letterhead, or PDF.** Status colors exist for success/warning/error/info feedback in the advisor / family-office tooling. **They are semantic-only — never use a status color as a decorative or brand accent**; a status hue must always mean its state.

Heritage status colors are calibrated to sit on the deep navy ground without clashing with the wax oxblood or bronze. Backgrounds are deeper, more muted than typical web defaults.

| Status | Background (on dark) | Text / Icon | Border |
|--------|---------------------|-------------|--------|
| Success | `hsl(150 24% 16%)` | `hsl(150 40% 72%)` | `hsl(150 24% 30%)` |
| Warning | `hsl(34 30% 20%)`  | `hsl(34 50% 74%)`  | `hsl(34 30% 36%)` |
| Error   | `hsl(8 36% 22%)`   | `hsl(8 50% 74%)`   | `hsl(8 36% 36%)` |
| Info (uses Steel Cobalt) | `hsl(214 32% 20%)` | `hsl(214 40% 76%)` | `hsl(214 38% 38%)` |

Info status and Steel Cobalt are the same hue family — this is intentional. Cobalt means "currently active / informational" everywhere it appears.

### Gain / loss — finance-specific semantic tokens

**Always declare gain and loss as their own semantic tokens, never hard-code green-up / red-down at the component level.** Some markets and accounting contexts invert the convention; some principals (especially those from non-US jurisdictions) read the convention differently. Map at the token tier so a single edit flips behavior globally.

| Token | Default mapping | Notes |
|-------|-----------------|-------|
| `--status-positive` | Success colors above | Always paired with an upward arrow or `+` prefix, never color alone. |
| `--status-negative` | Error colors above | Always paired with a downward arrow or `−` prefix and parentheses for negative amounts in tables. |
| `--status-flat` | Muted foreground | For unchanged / 0.00% rows. |

WCAG AA on body text minimum, AAA where the content is a fiduciary disclosure or a quarterly letter. **Never rely on color alone** to communicate gain, loss, or status — pair with icons, signs, parentheses, or labels.

### Visualization palette

Eight calibrated identity colors. Three permitted uses: (1) data visualization — chart, allocation pie, distribution-bar series fills; (2) identity markers — avatars, categorical tags tied to a specific entity (a trust, a beneficiary, a jurisdiction); (3) brand-decorative accents on long-form surfaces (a quarterly-letter section glyph, a quiet category marker in a portfolio statement). This is the accent palette — reach for it instead of a status color, used sparingly.

Never use viz colors on UI controls (buttons, alerts, status indicators, badges) — those have their own dedicated palettes above. Never use **Wax Oxblood**, **Bronze Hairline**, or **Steel Cobalt** as a chart series — they have other jobs and their identity meaning breaks the moment they become a chart fill.

**Chart series assignment.** Single-series charts (one line/area/bar set) use index 0 (Slate Indigo). Multi-series charts cycle through the colors in index order — 0, 1, 2 … 7 — using every color once before any reuse.

Use the **base** color for solid fills, icons, or chart series; use the **background tint** for cards, detail panels, or grouped backgrounds tied to the same category.

| Index | Name | Base | Background tint (on dark) | Foreground on base |
|-------|------|------|---------------------------|--------------------|
| 0 | Slate Indigo  | `hsl(218 30% 48%)` | `hsl(218 22% 18%)` | light — `hsl(40 50% 92%)` |
| 1 | Tarnished Bronze | `hsl(34 28% 52%)` | `hsl(34 22% 18%)` | dark — `hsl(216 57% 13%)` |
| 2 | Iron Plum     | `hsl(340 18% 44%)` | `hsl(340 14% 18%)` | light — `hsl(40 50% 92%)` |
| 3 | Forest Moss   | `hsl(150 22% 36%)` | `hsl(150 16% 18%)` | light — `hsl(40 50% 92%)` |
| 4 | Linen Sand    | `hsl(40 30% 64%)`  | `hsl(40 22% 18%)`  | dark — `hsl(216 57% 13%)` |
| 5 | Harbour Blue  | `hsl(200 32% 50%)` | `hsl(200 22% 18%)` | light — `hsl(40 50% 92%)` |
| 6 | Heather       | `hsl(280 16% 50%)` | `hsl(280 14% 18%)` | light — `hsl(40 50% 92%)` |
| 7 | Quiet Fog     | `hsl(216 8% 56%)`  | `hsl(216 6% 18%)`  | dark — `hsl(216 57% 13%)` |

**Foreground rule.** Base colors at L < 50% (Slate Indigo, Iron Plum, Forest Moss, Harbour Blue, Heather) take light foreground text (`hsl(40 50% 92%)`); base colors at L ≥ 50% (Tarnished Bronze, Linen Sand, Quiet Fog) take dark foreground (`hsl(216 57% 13%)`). Verify per-color against WCAG AA. Index 7 (Quiet Fog) is the low-chroma "uncategorized / unknown" slot.

**Avatar / entity color assignment — cycle through all 8 before any reuse.** Assign by the entity's **stable position** in the list (e.g. sorted by creation order), `index = position % 8`. This guarantees the first 8 entities each get a distinct color and only repeats once the palette is exhausted. Do **not** assign by hashing the name (`hash(name) % 8`) — that collides even with a handful of entities and makes two unrelated trusts read as "the same."

**More than 8 categories — cycle with lightening.**

For visualizations requiring more than 8 categorical colors, cycle back through the palette using lightened, desaturated versions of the original colors:

```css
color-mix(in srgb, hsl(var(--color-viz-N)) 60%, white)
```

or equivalently: increase L by ~15 percentage points (cap at 85%) and reduce S by ~40% (multiply by 0.6). The two cycles render with the same hues but the second pass reads as visibly softer, keeping cycle-1 and cycle-2 distinguishable while preserving brand consistency.

## Decorative motifs

Heritage has exactly **three** sanctioned decorative devices. All three come straight from the wax-seal Zoom card. Nothing else is invented or imported.

### 1. The wax seal (the signature)

The HS wax seal is **the** Heritage motif. It appears in the lockup, in the upper-left of every letterhead, as the opening device on every PDF section break, and as the favicon. It is the closest thing the brand has to a watermark — pressed, irregular, deeper at the edges, never flat-drawn.

**When to use:**
- **In the lockup**, always paired with the wordmark.
- **Letterhead upper-left**, sitting alone above the salutation, at ~48–64px wide.
- **PDF section openers**, at the start of a chapter or quarterly letter, at ~32–48px.
- **The favicon**, at 32px square. Below 32px, the wax detail dissolves — use the hand-set "HS" monogram instead.

**When NOT to use:**
- Never as a generic bullet, list marker, or step indicator. The seal is not a checkmark.
- Never animated. It does not spin, fade in, pulse, or "stamp down" on page load.
- Never inside a button, badge, or chip. The seal is identity, not chrome.
- Never recolored. It is always Wax Oxblood. There is no "white version," no "navy version," no "single-color version" — the seal is the seal.
- Never set inside a containing shape. No ring, no card behind it.
- Never more than one wax seal per viewport. Multiple seals dilute the signature.

### 2. The luminous arc (the signature device)

A single hairline silver/cream arc, swept diagonally across the lower half of the page — drawn directly from the Zoom card's gleam of light across water. It is the brand's secondary signature, used **once per viewport at most**, very low opacity, never animated, never repeated, never interrupted.

**When to use:**
- **Hero sections** — a single arc sweeping from the lower-left toward the upper-right, at 8–15% opacity, never intersecting the wordmark or the wax seal.
- **PDF cover pages** — a single arc beneath the title, at 10% opacity.
- **Section dividers between two major page chapters** — a single short arc fragment replacing a horizontal rule.

**When NOT to use:**
- Never on light surfaces — the arc is a gleam of light on a dark sea; it disappears on parchment.
- Never inside cards, panels, or any content surface.
- Never more than one per visible viewport. Two arcs are not more brand than one.
- Never animated. It does not sweep in, fade up, or follow the cursor.
- Never tinted bronze or oxblood. The arc is silver/cream — `#d4d8df` Stone Mist at 8–15% opacity, or a soft `hsl(0 0% 100% / 0.12)`.
- Never used as a section spine or as a divider line at standard rule weight — that role belongs to the bronze hairline.

### 3. The bronze hairlines (top + bottom structural rule)

Two non-negotiable structural elements on every full-page surface (marketing pages, deck slides, PDF covers):

- **Top:** a 1px bronze hairline running the full width directly beneath the primary nav (or beneath the wordmark on a deck slide / PDF cover).
- **Bottom:** a 1px bronze hairline running the full width of the page, flush with the bottom edge, **above** the footer text (not below). The rule frames the page like the top and bottom margins of a private placement memorandum.

These two rules frame every Heritage surface like the borders of a leather-bound minute book or the chrome of a quarterly letter cover. They are the brand's chrome. Note: Heritage uses two **hairlines**, not the thick bottom bar JetHQ uses — this is intentional, because the firm's voice is quieter than a brokerage's voice. The bronze itself is thin enough at any size to read as ink-on-page.

## Layout

### Grid

12-column grid, 24px gutter at desktop, 16px at tablet, 8px at mobile. Container max-widths:

- Editorial marketing: 1240px outer, 720px text measure for long-form prose.
- App / advisor tooling: 1440px outer, full-bleed schedules and registers inside.
- PDF / deck slide: 1140px (matches a standard 16:9 deck export at 1920×1080 with margins).
- Letterhead / quarterly letter: 680px text column on an 850px parchment page.

### Page shell

Every full-page surface follows the same shell:

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  [HS seal + wordmark]      Mark · Type · Color · Visual  │  ← nav, 80px tall
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤  ← 1px bronze hairline
│                                                          │
│                      [page content]                      │
│              (single luminous arc, 10% opacity)          │
│                                                          │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤  ← 1px bronze hairline
│  Heritage Strategies LLC · est.   ·   svnboet…ar.gh       │  ← footer text, 13px
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The nav, the top bronze hairline, the bottom bronze hairline, and the footer text are present on **every** marketing surface and PDF cover. Internal app pages may drop the footer in favor of a denser status bar, but never drop the bronze hairlines.

### Spacing

Base unit: **8px**. Spacing tokens: 4, 8, 12, 16, 24, 32, 48, 64, 96, 128, 192. Marketing layouts bias toward 48–192 — Heritage breathes more than JetHQ because the editorial register requires it. App layouts use 8–24 most often.

### Border-radius

Disciplined small set: `0` (table cells, hairline dividers, document interiors), `4px` (cards, inputs, buttons), `8px` (modals, large panels), `9999px` (status badges, avatars only — never primary buttons). Heritage buttons are rectangles with a 4px radius, not pills. Pills read as consumer-app casual and break the trustee posture.

### Elevation

Almost flat. Default surfaces use a 1px border, no shadow. Cards on dark surfaces are darker, not lighter. Inputs add `box-shadow: 0 1px 1px 0 rgba(0, 0, 0, 0.18);` to register as fields on the dark ground. Modals and dropdowns get a single soft elevation:

```css
box-shadow: 0 16px 40px -12px rgba(0, 0, 0, 0.55);
```

Never use heavy drop shadows — they read as dated and salesy.

### Motion

Fast 150ms, base 250ms, slow 400ms; ease-out for fades, ease-in-out for transforms. No bounce, no spring, no parallax, no decorative motion. The luminous arc never animates — not on scroll, not on hover, not on page load. Honor `prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` with a zero-duration variant.

```css
@media (prefers-reduced-motion: reduce) {
  *, *::before, *::after {
    animation-duration: 0ms !important;
    transition-duration: 0ms !important;
  }
}
```

## Imagery

The single biggest mistake firms in this category make is generic stock — skylines, handshakes, glass towers, sunset over a mountain, "advisor reviewing portfolio on tablet at conference table." Heritage does not use them. Imagery is either:

1. **Archival document specimens** — a redacted page from a 1968 trust schedule, a stylized fragment of an estate distribution chart, a flow diagram closeup of a multi-jurisdictional family entity map, the front of a quarterly letter as a portrait. Heritage's own artifacts, treated as the imagery.
2. **Distinctive abstract textural art** — the navy-cloud-with-luminous-arc backdrop from the wax-seal Zoom card is the canonical example. Texture, gradient depth, and a quiet horizon line. Oil-on-canvas restraint. No illustrated icons of people, no flat vector scenes.
3. **Commissioned, restrained portraiture** — real principals, real advisors, real ranch / coastline / library, shot with available light, no slick post-production, no model-stock smiles. Caption every image with the person's name and title.
4. **Vintage cartography and charts** — the back endpaper of a 1920s atlas, a quiet topographic map fragment, a hand-drawn isobar chart. Used as section background at low opacity, never as hero.

**The test:** could a competitor lift this image onto their own site? If yes, it is working against you. Replace it.

Stock wealth imagery is forbidden. If a photograph is required and no commissioned shot exists, default to the navy ground with the luminous arc — no image is better than the wrong image.

## Voice and page patterns

Marketing surfaces sell trust, not features. Concretely:

- **Hero copy must recognize the visitor in three seconds.** Speak to the specific situation — a generational business handoff, a liquidity event, concentrated or illiquid wealth, multi-jurisdictional families, a trustee transition. Do **not** open with "What is a multi-family office?" or "About Heritage." The visitor knows what they have; the question they are answering is *can I trust these people with it.*
- **Proof goes high on the page.** Named principals with real faces and credentials, years in operation, the shape of the families served (without naming them), regulatory affiliations shown as a quiet trust bar, one or two discreet narratives that demonstrate judgment without bragging.
- **One low-pressure call to action per page.** "Request a confidential conversation." Framed as discretion, not lead capture. A single field — name and email, or just a Calendly-style time picker. Never a ten-field form, never urgency, never a pop-up, never a chatbot.
- **No marketing motion.** No carousels. No animated AUM counters. No autoplay video. No exit-intent overlays. Each reads as salesy and erodes the trustee positioning.
- **Body voice — direct, declarative, owner/date/next-step framed.** No em dashes (Heritage tone, set in feedback — call this out explicitly if a draft contains one; use a comma, a period, parentheses, or a colon instead). No hedging. Short sentences sit comfortably next to long ones. **Never "we believe" or "we are passionate about" or "experience true wealth"** — show, never claim. Speak like a trustee writing a quarterly letter, not a marketer pitching a product.

### Specific banned phrases

These appear on every retail-wealth site. They do not appear on a Heritage surface, ever:

- "We believe..."
- "We are passionate about..."
- "Experience true wealth"
- "Your trusted partner in..."
- "Your wealth, your way"
- "Tailored to YOU"
- "Schedule a discovery call"
- "Unlock your financial future"
- "Empower your legacy"

If a draft contains any of these, rewrite. The trustee voice is declarative and concrete: "Heritage Strategies has been engaged by 47 families since 1998. Each engagement begins with a six-month listening period." That sentence does the work of a paragraph of marketing copy.

### App surfaces (advisor / family-office tooling)

App surfaces sell competence and care. Keep the same restraint, but allow density:

- Schedules, distribution charts, and asset registers are dense by necessity. Respect the density — do not pad data screens with marketing whitespace, and do not import editorial display type into app chrome.
- Surface only one primary action per screen. Secondary actions ghost. Destructive actions (release a distribution, close a trust, archive a beneficiary record) require a **typed confirmation** of the entity name, never just a "Delete" button at hover-distance.
- Audit trail and provenance are part of the brand. Show "last edited by [name] on [date]" on every editable artifact. Show "approved by [trustee] on [date]" on every distribution. This is how the product earns trust the way the firm does.

## Trust bar

Heritage marketing surfaces should carry a **quiet trust bar** near the hero — a single line of subdued credentials, never logos-soup. Suggested pattern:


- Never inflate the AUM. The principal will know.

## Do / Don't

### Do

- **Default to the deep navy ground.** `#0e1d33` is the page. The brand was designed for it.
- **Use exactly two fonts** — display serif and sans. JetBrains Mono is allowed for account references, code, and file numbers only.
- **Keep the bronze hairlines top and bottom.** They frame the page like a leather-bound minute book.
- **Open every section with the bronze eyebrow + bronze rule + serif heading + sans subheading.** This is the spine.
- **Reserve Steel Cobalt for state.** Active, selected, focus, info. Nothing else.
- **Use tabular figures in any dense data.** Quarterly letters, asset registers, distribution schedules.
- **Use old-style figures in editorial serif prose.** A quarterly letter that reads "across four generations" looks like a letter, not a spreadsheet.
- **Show the principal.** Named, with credentials. Real face, available light. Never centered-stock-smile.
- **Treat the wax seal as a signature** — once per viewport at most. The letterhead, the lockup, the section opener; never all three on the same page.
- **Treat the luminous arc as a watermark** — once per viewport, low opacity, never animated.

### Don't

- **Don't recolor the wax seal.** It is wax oxblood. There is no white version, no navy version, no single-color version.
- **Don't paint the wordmark all-oxblood or all-bronze.** Two-color treatment only: oxblood seal + cream wordmark on dark, oxblood seal + navy wordmark on light.
- **Don't omit the bronze hairline between HERITAGE and STRATEGIES LLC.** It is part of the wordmark, not a separator.
- **Don't paint a button oxblood by default.** Oxblood is identity. The filled-oxblood button is reserved for one hero CTA per page.
- **Don't paint a button bronze.** Bronze is a hairline color. It cannot carry a fill at any reasonable area without losing its metallic register.
- **Don't use cobalt as a decorative accent.** State only. The moment cobalt becomes ornament, its meaning is broken.
- **Don't make cards lighter than the page.** On dark, cards go darker — Quiet Slate `#091525`, not a lifted gray.
- **Don't use parchment as a marketing-page ground.** Parchment is for document interiors — letterhead, PDF body pages, print.
- **Don't use pure white anywhere except inside the lockup containment box or on a parchment-card.** Off-white parchment is the light surface.
- **Don't use stock wealth imagery.** Skylines, handshakes, glass towers, model-stock smiles at conference tables. If no commissioned shot exists, use the navy ground.
- **Don't introduce status colors on marketing surfaces.** Status is for app state.
- **Don't use vendor Tailwind colors** (`bg-amber-50`, `text-emerald-600`, `bg-blue-900`). They will not match the calibrated palette and will clash with the deep navy ground.
- **Don't add chrome textures, glassmorphism, animated counters, autoplay video, urgency banners, exit-intent overlays, or chatbots.** Every one reads as salesy.
- **Don't use pills for buttons.** Buttons are 4px-radius rectangles. Pills are reserved for status indicators and avatars.
- **Don't break the page frame.** Top bronze hairline and bottom bronze hairline are present on every full-page surface.
- **Don't write "we believe," "we are passionate about," or "experience true wealth."** Show, never claim. Speak like a trustee.
- **Don't use em dashes.** Heritage tone — set in feedback. Use a comma, a period, parentheses, or a colon instead.
- **Don't animate the wax seal or the luminous arc.** They are signatures, not motion graphics.
- **Don't use more than one wax seal or one luminous arc per visible viewport.** Two of either is dilution, not emphasis.

## Common mistakes to avoid

1. **Recoloring the wax seal.** It is wax oxblood, always. A navy seal, a white seal, or a flat-vector outlined seal is not the Heritage seal. The seal is the brand's family crest pressed into wax — recoloring it is the same as recoloring a coat of arms.
2. **Omitting the bronze hairline between HERITAGE and STRATEGIES LLC.** The hairline is part of the wordmark. Without it, the wordmark reads as two stacked words, not as an integrated mark.
3. **Putting cards on a lighter background than the page.** On dark, cards descend toward black, not ascend toward white. Inverting this kills the ink-on-paper feel that the brand depends on.
4. **Using cobalt as a brand color.** Cobalt is state. Painting a hero section cobalt or using cobalt as a chart series default breaks the brand's most reserved system color.
5. **Adding a third font.** A "modern" sans for nav and Inter for body is two fonts too many. Display serif + sans, full stop.
6. **Using parchment as a marketing-page ground.** Parchment is for the inside of a letterhead, a quarterly letter, a PDF page. It is not the home page. The home page is deep navy.
7. **Filling a button with bronze.** Bronze is a hairline color. A bronze-fill button reads as a 2014 web design; the bronze loses its metallic ink quality and becomes a flat brown. Use bronze as a 1px border only.
8. **Using stock wealth imagery.** A sunset-over-mountain hero shot, a handshake-on-glass-tower, an "advisor at conference table" stock will instantly drag the brand back into the retail-bank category it works to escape.
9. **Centering everything.** Heritage favors asymmetric balance — the wax seal sits to the upper-left, the body of the letter begins where it begins. Avoid the centered-stack landing page.
10. **Using "we believe" or "experience true wealth."** These are the banned phrases. They make the brand sound like every other retail-wealth marketing site. Rewrite to declarative, concrete, owner/date/next-step framed prose.
11. **Animating the wax seal or the luminous arc.** Both are signatures. Signatures do not animate. A signature that spins, fades up, or follows the cursor reads as a 2010 splash page.
12. **Using em dashes.** Heritage tone has no em dashes. Use a comma, a period, parentheses, or a colon. This is a specific feedback preference and a brand voice marker.

## Rules summary

- **Apply only what the content needs.** Marketing pages don't need status colors. Static documents don't need form chrome. Pull only the sections this output requires.
- **Two-color logo, always.** Wax-oxblood seal + cream wordmark on dark, wax-oxblood seal + navy wordmark on light. Never stretched, rotated, recolored, or boxed.
- **Two fonts, always.** Display serif (Tiempos / Cormorant Garamond) + sans (Söhne / Inter). No exceptions. Mono is allowed for account refs and code only.
- **Oxblood is identity, not interaction.** Use for the wax seal, the section-opening pip, the active-nav-item underline. Buttons are bronze-outline by default; filled-oxblood is reserved for one hero CTA per page.
- **Bronze is a hairline color.** Used in 1px rules and as the small-caps eyebrow color. Never as a fill.
- **Cobalt is state, not decoration.** Active, selected, focus, info. Nothing else.
- **Dark ground is the default.** Deep Midnight Navy `#0e1d33` is the page; cards go darker, not lighter. Parchment is for documents only.
- **The wax seal is the signature.** Once per viewport at most. Always wax oxblood. Never recolored, never animated, never inside a containing shape.
- **The luminous arc is a watermark.** Once per viewport, low opacity, never animated.
- **Bronze hairlines top and bottom.** Structural frame on every full-page surface.
- **Tabular figures in any dense data.** Quarterly letters, asset registers, distribution schedules.
- **Old-style figures in editorial serif prose.** Quarterly letters and family-office briefings read as letters, not spreadsheets.
- **No vendor Tailwind colors.** Use the calibrated values from this brief.
- **Buttons are rectangles, 4px radius.** Pills are for status and avatars.
- **No salesy chrome.** No urgency banners, autoplay video, glassmorphism, animated counters, chatbots, pop-ups.
- **No em dashes.** Heritage tone.
- **No "we believe," "experience true wealth," "schedule a discovery call."** Show, never claim.
- **WCAG AA on all body text, AAA on fiduciary disclosures and quarterly letters.** Verify per-color, especially Stone Mist on the navy ground.

## One-line summary

Heritage is **deep midnight navy ground + wax-oxblood identity seal + bronze hairlines + Tiempos / Cormorant serif + Söhne / Inter sans + a single luminous arc + steel cobalt reserved for state**. Restraint over volume. Heraldic over heroic. The brand is the stewardship, not the strategy.
